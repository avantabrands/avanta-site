The evaluation scorecard that matters most

A real TikTok Shop agency should be able to show how the account moves from setup to signal to scale. That means the team can explain how they handle creator recruitment, sample logistics, offer positioning, listing conversion work, paid amplification, and reporting without leaving critical pieces undefined.

1. Proof that shows the full system

Look for case studies and screenshots that show more than one metric. Useful proof includes GMV, order volume, affiliate contribution, paid efficiency, and store-growth views that line up with each other.

2. Clear channel ownership

If the agency only owns ads or only owns creators, ask who handles the operational gaps. TikTok Shop usually stalls when nobody owns listings, approval flow, creator follow-up, or conversion work.

3. Creator system depth

The right partner should explain how they recruit creators, brief them, follow up, replace weak output, and turn early wins into repeatable content volume.

4. Reporting that drives decisions

Reporting should tell you what to scale, what to cut, and what to test next. If reports stop at vanity numbers, the account usually stays noisy for too long.

Red flags to catch early

Brands lose time when they hire a partner that sounds strong in sales conversations but cannot translate that into channel operations. These warning signs tend to show up before the work even starts.

  • No screenshots, case studies, or explanation of what actions led to the results
  • Heavy emphasis on strategy without naming the operators who run creators, listings, and paid
  • No process for replacing weak creator output or refreshing top-performing hooks
  • Reporting focused on reach or views while conversion and GMV signals stay vague
  • Generic ecommerce language that ignores the realities of TikTok Shop buying behavior
Simple rule: If an agency cannot explain how creators, listings, and paid amplification work together, the growth story is probably thinner than it sounds.

Questions worth asking on the first call

The strongest agency conversations get concrete quickly. You are trying to understand how the team thinks once the channel gets messy, not whether they can describe the category in broad terms.

What do you own each week?

Have them name the weekly responsibilities across creator outreach, approvals, listing updates, testing, paid amplification, and reporting.

How do you find the first winners?

They should explain how they create early signal, what they measure, and how they decide which creators, offers, or product pages deserve more budget.

What changes when the account is underperforming?

Look for a specific troubleshooting model instead of generic reassurance. Strong operators can describe likely bottlenecks and the order they would fix them in.

Where do brands usually get stuck?

This answer often reveals whether the agency understands the operational density of TikTok Shop or only the surface-level media angle.

The 10-question due diligence checklist

Before signing with any TikTok Shop agency, run through this checklist on the final call. Strong answers move you forward. Red flag answers should trigger more digging or a hard pass.

1. "Show me a case study with live screenshots."

Good answer: Timestamped Seller Center dashboards, GMV by week, affiliate contribution breakdown, before/after listing views. Red flag: Only PDF slide decks with self-reported numbers and no verifiable source.

2. "Who on your team will actually work on my account?"

Good answer: Named individuals with defined roles — creator manager, listing specialist, paid strategist. Red flag: "Our team" with no specifics, or a senior pitch person who disappears after signing.

3. "How many active creator relationships do you have right now?"

Good answer: A specific number with a description of how they maintain quality and replace underperformers. Red flag: Vague claims of "thousands" without any explanation of how they manage or vet them.

4. "What is your process when a product does not sell in the first 30 days?"

Good answer: A clear diagnostic — listing audit, creator brief review, hook testing, offer repositioning. Red flag: "We give it more time" or shifting blame to the product category.

5. "How does your reporting connect to revenue decisions?"

Good answer: Weekly dashboards showing GMV by source, affiliate contribution, ROAS, and what the team is changing based on the data. Red flag: Reporting that shows impressions and views but never answers "what are we doing differently next week?"

6. "Who owns the creative assets you produce?"

Good answer: Brand retains full ownership of all deliverables including creator content licensed through them. Red flag: Vague "usage rights" language without clear channel permissions or time limits.

7. "What does the exit look like if it is not working?"

Good answer: A defined notice period (30–60 days), data handoff including creator records and campaign history, no clawback on GMV earned. Red flag: Penalties for early exit, no transition support, or data that "lives in our systems."

8. "Have you worked in my category before?"

Good answer: Named brands in the same vertical with results at a similar AOV and price point. Red flag: "We work across all categories" with no specific examples in yours.

9. "What is included in the fee vs what costs extra?"

Good answer: Line-by-line breakdown — creator samples, ad spend, content production, tool access, additional SKUs. Red flag: A retainer quote with no scope definition and an assumption that "everything is included."

10. "Are there performance milestones in the contract?"

Good answer: Defined GMV targets by month, with a remediation process if they are missed. Red flag: A contract that defines only deliverables (creator outreach, reports) but never outcomes.

The agency confidence signal: Agencies that have performed well welcome hard questions. Agencies that deflect, pivot to testimonials, or get defensive about contract terms are telling you something important before the engagement even starts.

What a healthy agency contract looks like

Most brands spend more time negotiating the monthly fee than the contract terms that actually protect them. This is backwards. The fee matters, but the contract structure determines who controls the outcome, who owns the work, and what happens when things go wrong.

A well-structured TikTok Shop agency contract covers four areas that most brands never think to ask about until they are already in a bad situation.

Performance clauses

The contract should define what "working" means — GMV milestones by month, creator activation targets, ROAS floors for paid spend. If the agency misses those milestones, there should be a defined remediation path (scope change, credit, or mutual termination) rather than an open-ended retry with no accountability.

Reporting cadence

Reporting frequency and format should be written into the agreement. Weekly GMV dashboards, monthly strategic reviews, and quarterly benchmark reports give you the visibility you need to catch underperformance early. Agencies that resist defined reporting cadence are usually hiding something — slow progress, attribution issues, or capacity gaps.

IP and creative ownership

Every piece of creative produced during the engagement — video assets, product briefs, creator templates, listing copy — should be owned by the brand at termination. "Usage rights" language is not sufficient. Specify that all deliverables transfer to the brand, including creator performance data, outreach records, and campaign history files.

Exit terms

The contract should include termination for convenience (you can leave with 30–60 days' notice without proving breach), termination for cause (defined as missing performance milestones for two consecutive months), and a mandatory transition period during which the agency hands over all data, creator contacts, and active campaign settings.

Brands that skip this level of contract scrutiny often find themselves 90 days in with underperforming results, no data they can take with them, and a lock-in clause that keeps them paying for another quarter. How to evaluate agency case studies

A case study is only as strong as the evidence underneath it. The industry has a wide range — from live Seller Center screenshots with specific attribution breakdowns to PDF slide decks that claim "$2M GMV" with no context, no timeline, and no explanation of what drove the result.

Here is how to distinguish credible from self-reported.

Credible case study signals

Live dashboard screenshots with visible timestamps. GMV figures broken down by source (organic, affiliate, paid). A clear before/after timeline showing what changed and when. Affiliate contribution percentage alongside paid ROAS. Specific product category and AOV context. An honest description of what did not work before finding the winning approach.

Red flag case study patterns

Single-metric claims ("grew GMV 400%") with no baseline. No timeline — you cannot tell if results took 2 weeks or 2 years. Screenshot-free — results described only in summary text. No explanation of what execution drove the outcome. Category, product type, and AOV never mentioned. Testimonial quotes standing in for data.

The creator concentration question: In most high-performing TikTok Shop accounts, a small number of creators produce most of the GMV. Ask any agency how many creators in a program like yours stayed productive for 60+ days. If the pitch jumps straight to volume claims ("we recruited 500 creators"), ask what percentage were still active at 90 days and what percentage drove more than 10% of GMV each. That is where real creator system depth shows.

Pricing model comparison: what works for brands at each stage

The pricing model an agency offers tells you something about how they think about the relationship. Each model creates a different incentive structure — and the right one depends on where you are in your TikTok Shop journey.

Flat monthly retainer ($2,500–$15,000/month)

How it works: Fixed fee regardless of GMV generated. The agency earns the same whether the account grows or stalls. Best for: Brands that need full-service management and want predictable budgeting. Works when the agency has a strong track record and the brand can hold them accountable to defined deliverables. Risk: Without performance clauses, there is no financial consequence for underperformance beyond reputational damage.

Commission on GMV (5–15% of sales)

How it works: Agency earns a percentage of the GMV they generate. No base fee or a minimal one. Best for: Brands with proven products and existing TikTok Shop traction who want to scale without a large fixed overhead. Risk: Requires clean attribution — the agency and brand need to agree on exactly which sales count. Can also incentivize volume over profitability if commission is applied to gross GMV regardless of margin.

Performance/milestone-based

How it works: Fee tied to hitting specific GMV targets — often structured as a base that unlocks additional payment once targets are met. Best for: Brands that want maximum incentive alignment and are willing to pay a premium for it. The most confident agencies offer this because they believe in their system. Risk: Rare in the market. Agencies that cannot perform avoid this model entirely — so its presence is itself a positive signal.

Hybrid (base retainer + performance bonus)

How it works: A lower base fee covers the agency's fixed costs (team time, tools) with a bonus tier that kicks in above defined GMV milestones. Best for: Most established brands — it balances predictability for the brand with upside incentive for the agency. Risk: Bonus thresholds need to be set carefully; too low and the agency earns full bonus for mediocre results, too high and the performance component becomes meaningless.

Frequently asked questions

What should a TikTok Shop agency actually own?+
A strong TikTok Shop agency should own the work that most often blocks growth: creator recruitment, affiliate activation, listing optimization, content coordination, paid amplification, and weekly reporting tied to revenue signals. The key test is whether the agency takes ownership of outcomes, not just activities.
What proof should a brand ask for before hiring a TikTok Shop agency?+
Ask for live Seller Center screenshots with timestamps, GMV broken down by source (organic, affiliate, paid), and a clear explanation of what execution decisions drove the results. Credible proof shows a timeline — what changed and when — not just a final number. Avoid relying on PDF summaries without verifiable data.
When is a specialized TikTok Shop agency a better fit than a general ecommerce agency?+
A specialized TikTok Shop agency is usually the better fit when growth depends on creator pipelines, affiliate operations, listing conversion work, and paid amplification built around social-commerce behavior. General ecommerce agencies rarely have the creator relationships, Seller Center expertise, or GMV Max experience needed to compete on TikTok Shop specifically.
What should a TikTok Shop agency contract include?+
At minimum: defined performance milestones with a remediation process if missed, a reporting cadence written into the agreement, IP and creative ownership that transfers fully to the brand at exit, and termination-for-convenience clauses with a 30–60 day notice period. Data portability — including creator records, campaign history, and reporting views — should be guaranteed in writing.
What is the difference between retainer, commission, and performance-based agency pricing?+
Retainer pricing is a fixed monthly fee regardless of results — predictable for budgeting but misaligned on incentives. Commission pricing ties agency earnings to a percentage of GMV (typically 5–15%), which aligns incentives but requires clean attribution. Performance-based pricing ties fees to hitting specific revenue milestones — the most aligned model, rare because only confident operators offer it. The hybrid model (base retainer plus a GMV performance bonus) is the most common structure at established agencies.
